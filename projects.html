Current Postdoc

Uncovering the genomic diversity, lifestyles and evolutionary origins of the DPANN archaea

I am currently working as a Postdoc in the lab of Dr. Anja Spang at the Royal Netherlands Institute for Sea Research (NIOZ) in the Netherlands.

Here, I investigate archaea using metagenomic approaches.

Archaea make up one of the main divisions of life, next to the Bacteria and the Eukaryotes and live in all habitats on Earth ranging from soils and sediments to marine and freshwater environments as well as the human gut.

Metagenomics is a technique in which DNA is sequenced directly from environmental samples and DNA fragments used to reconstruct genomes from uncultivated organisms. This technique has not only revealed that microbial life is much more diverse than originally thought, but also shed light on what the role of these microbes is in their habitats In the archaea this approach lead to the description of a large diversity of putative symbiotic organisms, most of which belong to the archaeal superphylum referred to as DPANN archaea.

Members of the DPANN include organisms with very small genomes and limited metabolic capabilities, which suggests that these organisms depend on other microbes for growth and survival. Consistent with that, the few so far cultivated DPANN archaea are obligate symbionts or parasites that cannot live on their own.

I am interested in gaining insights into the biology and evolution of DPANN archaea using metagenomic approaches. Specifically, I am interested in:

  • Understanding how diverse DPANN archaea are in marine environments
  • Develop approaches that allow us to identify the interaction partners of DPANN archaea
  • Finding the position of DPANN in the archaeal tree of life and addressing the question, whether symbiosis evolved early in the archaeal domain

Find out more:

  • Genomic diversity, lifestyles and evolutionary origins of DPANN archaea
  • Talking about a novel phylum in the DPANN archaea


Investigating the microbial diversity and nutrient dependencies of microbes in the Black Sea

The Black Sea is a stratified, anaerobic marine environment and as such provides extremely stable conditions making it an ideal study site. Using metagenomic approaches we want to compare microbial communities at different depths and untangle the nutrient interdependencies of microbes in the water column and the sediment. Our preliminary data suggests that DPANN archaea are extremely diverse and abundant in the Black Sea. Therefore, we want to use this dataset to build methods to predict the host of these DPANN archaea based on sequencing data.


Postdoc at the MSI

Understanding the diversity and metabolic potential of hydrothermal vent-associated micobes

During my Postdoc at the Marine Science Institute (University of Texas) in the lab of Dr. Brett Baker I reconstructed microbial genomes and resolved metabolic capabilities of microbes found in hydrothermal sediment vent sediments.

Specifically, I was looking asking:

  • How diverse are microbial communities found in Guaymas Basin deep-sea hydrothermal vent sediments?
  • What is the genetic, functional and metabolic potential encoded in bacteria genomes from these sediment communities?

Find out more:

  • Expansive microbial metabolic versatility and biodiversity in dynamic Guaymas Basin hydrothermal sediments


Reconstructing genomes from microbes involved in oil degradation

In another project, I identified hydrocarbon degradation pathways in genomes from uncultured bacteria from water affected by the Deep Water Horizon Oil Spill. My research questions were:

  • What were the hydrocarbon degrading bacteria that were present during the Deepwater Horizon oil spill
  • What pathways do these bacteria employ for oil-degradation?
  • What features can we find in their genomes that allow them quickly to adapt to a high oil input?

Find out more:

  • Reconstructing metabolic pathways of hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria from the Deepwater Horizon oil spill
  • Can we harness bacteria to help clean up future oil spills?


Previous Research

PhD

What microbes are found close to plant roots and how do they affect plant growth?

I did my doctoral Research (2012-2015) in the lab of Prof. Schulze-Lefert at the Max-Planck-Institute for Plant Breeding Research in Cologne, Germany. Here, I worked on two main research topics:

  1. Analyse the structure and function of the bacterial root microbiota of Arabidopsis thaliana and relative species. Here, I asked:
  • What is the structure of bacterial communities found in natural soil, rhizosphere and plant roots of Arabis alpina, Arabidopsis thaliana and Cardamine hirsuta?
  • What are factors (i.e. environment, soil type, flowering, …) that shape the assembly of bacterial communities?

Find out more:

  • Root microbiota dynamics of perennial Arabis alpina are dependent on soil residence time but independent of flowering time
  • Quantitative divergence of the bacterial root microbiota in Arabidopsis thaliana relatives

  1. Cultivate root-associated rhizobia and study their plant growth-promoting properties on Arabidopsis thaliana.
  • How diverse is the Rhizobia population found on roots of Arabidopsis thaliana?
  • What is the role of root-associated Rhizobia on plant growth?
  • How do Rhizobia affect the plant transcriptome?

Find out more:

  • Modular Traits of the Rhizobiales Root Microbiota and Their Evolutionary Relationship with Symbiotic Rhizobia


Masters

Studying auxin-inducible genes in Arabidobsis thaliana

I did my undergraduate Research (2010-2011) in the QuintLab at the Leibniz Institute for Plant Biochemistry in Halle, Germany, and wrote my thesis on Ribosomal mRNA profiling of auxin-inducible genes in Arabidopsis thaliana.

Here, I:

  • Established of translating ribosomal affinity purification (TRAP) as new experimental technique in order to isolate cell-type specific mRNA as well as development of new cell-type-specific maker lines
  • Investigated of the effect of auxin application on the expression of marker genes in different root-cell types
